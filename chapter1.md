# Learning to Code

## Introduction: The First Line

Every program ever written began the same way: with a single line typed by someone who did not yet know how it would end. That is a more comforting thought than it might first appear. The developers whose work you rely on every day—the people who built the apps on your phone, the sites you shop on, the systems that move your money from one account to another—all started as beginners staring at a blank screen, unsure of what to type. Coding is not a talent you are born with or without. It is a craft, and like every craft, it is learned one small, deliberate step at a time. This book exists to walk those steps with you.

You will not find shortcuts here, because there are none worth taking. What you will find instead is a way of thinking: how to break a large, intimidating problem into pieces small enough to solve, how to describe those pieces in a language a computer can follow, and how to recover gracefully when—not if—something goes wrong. Errors are not signs of failure. They are the ordinary texture of programming, the way a musician plays a wrong note on the way to playing the right one. The goal of these chapters is not to help you memorize commands but to help you build the kind of patient, curious mindset that turns confusion into understanding, and understanding into working software.

So begin without fear. You do not need an expensive machine, a computer science degree, or permission from anyone to start. You need curiosity, a willingness to be wrong on the way to being right, and the discipline to show up and try again tomorrow. Read actively, type the examples yourself rather than merely reading them, and treat every exercise as a small experiment rather than a test. By the end of this book you will not just know how to write code—you will know how to teach yourself whatever comes next, which is the only skill in this field that never goes out of date. Turn the page, and let's write that first line together.
